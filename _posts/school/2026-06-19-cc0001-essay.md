## Transit Treasures: Art Appreciation in a Fast-Paced Society

![The Crowd of Commuters at Dhoby Ghaut MRT During Rush Hour]({{site.baseurl}}/assets/images/cc0001-pic-1.jpg "The Crowd of Commuters at Dhoby Ghaut MRT During Rush Hour")
**Figure 1:** The Crowd of Commuters at Dhoby Ghaut MRT During Rush Hour

A faint woosh hums through the platform. Two eyes of light emerge from the distance, accompanied by the crescendoing clatter of the Alstom R151’s wheels. The train screeches to a halt. The glass doors open. Commuters rush to the swiftly turning escalators like a turbulent stream. On the concourse, alighting passengers merge with hurried steps. A cacophony of footsteps reverberates against the granite floor. A card reader beeps. A fare gate swings open. A commuter rushes toward the closing train doors. His eyebrows furrow. A “tsk” of frustration escapes from his breath. I can feel the agony of waiting two minutes for the next train.

![A Dhoby Washing Clothes Juxtaposed With Modern Landmarks]({{site.baseurl}}/assets/images/cc0001-pic-2.png "A Dhoby Washing Clothes Juxtaposed With Modern Landmarks")
**Figure 2:** A Dhoby Washing Clothes Juxtaposed With Modern Landmarks

In contrast, still pieces of art bear witness to the fleeting movements in the surroundings: One depicting the rocks and leaves of the earth on towering gypsum columns, another illustrating a Dhoby surrounded by malls, hotels, and offices with the words “Dhoby Ghaut” in a cartoon art style. On the floor above, artists sit at pop-up booths, patiently waiting for hours on end for customers to pass by from nearby food stalls. They await the opportunity to share their art prints of imaginary road trips through vast cornfields. Despite the constant stream of workers passing by the art pieces and booths, the stream of footsteps ceases to halt. Not a single individual stops to look!

The unrelenting footsteps of the crowd and the frantic rush to beat closing doors both seem to indicate an acute awareness of the minute ebb and flow of time. Our present-day society enforces an artificial rhythm. Train schedules, office hours, and class timetables dictate our motions through space and time. We have become a society attuned to the swiftly turning hands of the clock. In a world of schedules and deadlines, of punctuality and productivity, our consciousness learned to discretise time to its smallest perceivable divisions.

The art installations featuring natural and historical elements seem to depict a slower time, far removed from the pace of present society. Artists seem content spending hours reflecting on their experiences, illustrating pieces to capture these, and waiting for curious passersby to stop and admire their works. Perhaps, in breaking free from the mechanical metronome of our fast-paced society, they realise authentic experiences—deep realities of humans, culture, history, and nature that cannot be gleaned from just a momentary look. This led me to wonder: How does the fast-paced nature of society influence how people view or value art?

In her master’s thesis, Guan (2023), an interior design graduate from the Pratt Institute, argues for the need to slow down from the fast pace of life due to work. She noted how art—particularly, museum art—enables us to slow down because it shows a slower pace of life in the past. In this way, art challenges us to reflect on our experiences and broader sociocultural values. This slow reflection creates what Guan calls a “relational experience” between an individual and an artwork, which “enables people to resonate with their surrounding world and explore individual emotion, human relations, and their social context” (Guan, 2023, pp. 6-7). Simply put, slow contemplation enables us to relate our lived experiences in terms of what the artwork depicts.

Art invites individuals to slow down and recontextualise their lived experiences in terms of the artwork’s cultural, historical, and humanist elements. It challenges us to explore the unseen, to step beyond our familiar world into the artist’s world. Guan (2023), however, does not address whether individuals today would initiate artistic contemplation in the first place. People nowadays seem not to be predisposed to contemplating historical art, as the apparent apathy of the commuters highlights.

On the surface, it seems that our fast-paced society inhibits such relational, contemplative experiences by reducing the time we have for contemplation. Daily worries, such as juggling leisure with work and family responsibilities, seem to inhibit contemplation. While plausible, this explanation fails to account for the sheer amount of time people engage with contemporary art. I, like many today, find myself spending hours deciphering the meaning of a film or disentangling the lyrics of a song. This behaviour is a stark contrast to the apparent lack of attention to the artworks in the MRT. What, then, imbues contemporary art forms with this appeal that historical art seems to lack?

A Channel News Asia (CNA) article by Sng (2025), a graduate of Communication Studies from Nanyang Technological University (NTU), highlights how art satisfies an individual’s desire to be remembered. Creative pursuits allow artists to unwind and reconnect with their emotions and values. Through this reconnection, individuals impress a part of themselves in their art. The art they create functions as a memento of their emotions and experiences. As Sng notes, “[writing poems] also helps me preserve my own personal history, and pushes me to keep pursuing new opportunities to write new chapters of my life story” (Sng, 2025, para. 31). Art records, enriches, and immortalises our lived experiences.

Sng (2025) reveals that art is our response to a deep-rooted desire to remember and be remembered. Art is a medium of recollection. Through it, we manifest our thoughts, feelings, and experiences into reality, enabling them to transcend the boundaries of time. I observed this in the pop-up stall owners in the MRT. Their willingness to wait for hours to sell their art perhaps signifies their desire to make their story heard and remembered. In turn, I found myself resonating with the stories told by this artist’s work. In contrast, I did not feel the same connection with the artworks in the MRT, finding them too unrelatable or abstract. Perhaps, then, the apparent apathy of the commuters stems from a lack of relatability with what the artworks depict.

Guan (2023) and Sng (2025) both agree that art functions as a tapestry of lived experiences. Individuals create art to immortalise their emotions and experiences—both past and present (Sng, 2025). Taken together, Guan and Sng reveal that art enables a perpetual cycle of unfolding dialogue through time. Past artists imbue art with their lived experiences (Sng, 2025). Art invites reflection on human existence and enriches future individuals’ lived experiences (Guan, 2023). Through the cycle of deliberate slow contemplation and creation, individuals continuously add to and enrich this tapestry of lived experiences over time.

Technological advancements made art accessible to a global audience. In our fast-paced society, many, like the artists in the pop-up stalls, turn to art as a means to slow down and record their feelings and experiences. The cross-temporal dialogue enabled by art remains seemingly prevalent today. However, the commuters’ attitude toward the artworks in the MRT leads me to question the integrity of this dialogue.

I initially conjectured that the lack of appreciation for the art in the MRT stemmed from a lack of time. The commuters moved like clockwork, a microcosm of the culture of efficiency Singaporeans find themselves in. In such a culture, perhaps individuals are too absorbed in everyday worries to appreciate art in their immediate environment. Now I realise that, beyond a lack of time, individuals gravitate toward contemporary and relatable art. Our fast-paced society has led us to value predictability, stability, and safety—including in the way we engage with art. We listen to love songs or watch films in our free time in part because we see in the characters, setting, or story a reflection of our lives. We seek familiarity and comfort in art. In contrast, the people and cultures depicted in historical art, like the painting of Dhobies in Dhoby Ghaut MRT, seem not to have struck a chord of relatability with the passersby. This should raise a concern—in neglecting historical art, we stand to lose a significant dimension of past human experiences and learnings.

Amidst our fast-paced society, the human desire to relate, create, and express remains. With these desires, art continues and will continue to be appreciated. Art, especially historical art, captures the richness and variety of human experiences across time. However, our fast-paced society has led us to seek the predictable and the familiar. In so doing, our world shrinks—we close ourselves off from the infinite depth and nuance of the human experience that such art depicts. Perhaps, then, the lack of appreciation of art was never the concern, but rather the lack of appreciation for the thoughts, feelings, ideas, and experiences expressed by past artists. We are at risk of forgetting what was. We are at risk of losing the chance to learn from the lives of those who lived before us. It is only when we willingly slow down and contemplate the unfamiliar that we can remember—that we can internalise—these past experiences to enrich our lives and those of others.
